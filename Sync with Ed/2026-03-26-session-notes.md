## Charting — Moving Averages (20, 50, 200 Day)

- Ed uses **20-day, 50-day, and 200-day moving averages** (learned from Investor's Business Daily)
- Each line represents the stock's average price over that period relative to the current price
- **When the lines intersect** = change in stock price direction. This is clockwork across any stock.
- When the stock price gets **below all three MAs** = heavy resistance/selling pressure. Very difficult for the stock to climb back over all three lines. "It takes one buyer at a time, one day at a time."
- When all three **line up on a 3-year weekly chart**, something big is about to happen.
- Use **weekly charts for long-term** view, **daily charts for shorter-term** confirmation.

## Charting — Support & Resistance

### Definitions
- **Support** = the bottom. Price level where algorithms by major funds/ETFs say "BUY." The floor.
- **Resistance** = the top. Price level where algorithms say "SELL." The ceiling.
- When price is between support and resistance with no action = "trading within a trend" — rubber band wound up, will eventually explode one direction.

### How to Find Support & Resistance
1. Start at the **lowest point** on the chart — that's your first support
2. Find the **next high** after that low — that's your first resistance
3. Continue: next low = new support, next high = new resistance
4. **Connect the dots** — the more bottoms/tops that line up on the same horizontal level, the more significant (major) that support/resistance is

### Major vs Minor
- **Major** = long-term trends with big volume at those levels. Multiple touch points across different time periods.
- **Minor** = shorter-term bounces, low volume. Stock quickly resumes trend.
- **Volume confirms** — if support/resistance happens on heavy volume, it's major. Light volume = minor.
- Ed changes line colors for major vs minor on his charts.

## Volume, Accumulation/Distribution, and Relative Strength (RSI)

### Volume
- Tracks number of transactions (which equals dollar amount)
- Look for **above-average volume** on price moves — confirms the move is real
- Price going UP on heavy volume = legit buying pressure
- Price going DOWN on heavy volume = legit selling pressure
- Low volume moves = less significant

### Accumulation/Distribution
- Shows whether people are **holding** (accumulating) or **selling** (distributing)
- High accumulation = supply & demand — fewer shares available = price bids up
- Low accumulation on rallies = weak, likely to reverse

### Relative Strength (RSI)
- Measures stock performance vs the entire market
- **Over 70** = stock is beating 70% of the market, breaking out, nothing but buyers
- **Under 50** = buyers disappearing, decline is real
- **Down to 35** = "Katie bar the doors" — massive selling
- Ed watches RSI to confirm trends before entering trades

## Options Chain Crash Course

### Delta
- For every $1 the stock moves in your direction, you make that delta in cents
- **50 delta** = at the money. $1 move = $0.50 gain. Ed normally trades 50-55 delta.
- **20 delta** = out of the money. $1 move = $0.20 gain. Used for smaller accounts ($1K).
- 20 delta = ~20% chance of finishing in the money
- Brandon's account ($1K) can't afford 50 delta options ($2,000+), so 20 delta ($635) fits the budget

### IV Rank (IVR)
- PLTR IVR was 18.7 at time of trade = **cheap options**
- Low IVR means buying low. If volatility expands, the option gains value from IV increase ON TOP of the directional move.
- Ed emphasized: "This is part of the green dots" in Go Maz rules

### Bid/Ask
- **Bid** = what you can sell for (lower number)
- **Ask** = what you have to pay to buy (higher number)
- Ed starts between bid and ask, slightly toward the bid side to save money
- "I pick up 50, 60, $70 just by working it" on higher volume trades

### Standard Deviation & Expected Move
- The line on the options chain represents **one standard deviation**
- 70% of the time, the stock ends up within this range
- Based on Black-Scholes model using last 30 days, 90 days, and 1 year of price action
- Ed assigned homework: study Black-Scholes, expected move, and standard deviation

### 5. Options Greeks Deep Dive

#### Theta Decay
- Buying an option = paying theta daily (it costs you)
- Selling an option = collecting theta daily ("being the bank")
- Accelerated theta decay curve makes it better to hold options longer than 60 DTE
- Ed's preferred approach: set options 120 days out, bet on longer-term chart trend, use stop losses
- Friend's losses driven by short-dated options (7-14 days) where theta is "out of control"
- **Reference:** Theta decay curve chart built for PLTR $120 PUT showing flat decay at 108 DTE, acceleration at 45 DTE, vertical decay in last 30 days

#### Delta
- 30-delta call = 30% chance of profit if bought, 70% chance if sold
- If you buy and are correct, you make 30 cents on the dollar (assuming gamma, vega, theta constant)
- Delta is a calculation related to standard deviation

#### Gamma
- The "accelerator" of delta — a number (not a dollar amount) that impacts delta
- Can cause a 50-delta to accelerate to 75-delta on a big price movement
- Net gamma = difference between call and put contracts
- Net delta is derived from net gamma
- Positive GEX (gamma exposure) = dealers long gamma = mean-reverting behavior, suppressed volatility
- **Reference:** Screenshot showing GEX +87,612,240.5, Net Delta +25,537,238,830.18 — "Positive GEX (dealers long gamma - mean-reverting, suppressed vol)"

### 7. Market Maker Mechanics
- Market makers aim for **100% delta neutral** — equal puts to calls = delta of zero
- They take the other side of trades when no counterparty exists
- They constantly rebalance to maintain delta neutrality
- This rebalancing process = how they suppress volatility = source of trading platform fees
- Recent sudden burst of orders forced market makers to catch up with buying → net delta increase of +25 based on gamma
